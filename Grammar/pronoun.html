Pronouns

A pronoun is used in the place of a noun. It substitutes the noun in a paragraph or piece of writing to avoid repetition of the noun. Pronouns can be used in singular and plural forms. The verb used in the sentence should be used in accordance with the particular form of the pronoun used.

Common pronouns are he, she, you, me, I, we, us, this, them, that.
A pronoun can act as a subject, direct object, indirect object, object of the preposition, and more and takes the place of any person, place, animal or thing.

    Repetition of nouns :

John loves to play soccer. John plays soccer every weekend with his friends. John's skills have improved a lot because John practices soccer regularly.

    Pronoun usage create smoother, more natural-sounding sentences :

John loves to play soccer. He plays every weekend with his friends. His skills have improved a lot because he practices regularly.

Types of Pronouns

Personal Pronouns :

Personal pronouns are used to refer to the person speaking or being spoken to.

Personal pronouns can change their form based on:

  • Person (first-, second-, or third-person)
  • Number (singular or plural)
  • Gender (masculine, feminine, neuter, or epicene)
  • Case (subject, object, possessive, or reflexive/intensive)

Demonstrative Pronouns :

Those used to point to something specific within a sentence. There are only four demonstrative pronouns – this, that, these, those – but the usage can be a bit tricky at times.

  • The “near” demonstrative this (singular) or these (plural) indicates something close to you.
  • The “far” demonstrative that (singular) or those (plural) indicates something farther from you.
  1. I prefer this.
  2. These are beautiful, but those belong to Danny.
  3. Did you see that?

Possessive Pronouns :

Possessive pronouns are used to indicate ownership or possession without repeating the noun.

Examples include: mine, its, hers, his, yours, ours, theirs, whose.

  1. This cat is mine .
  2. Are these bananas yours ?
  3. This money is ours .

Relative Pronouns :

Relative pronouns are used to indicate a relation between two nouns. They will usually appear after a noun to help clarify the sentence or give extra information.

Examples include: who, which, that, whom, whose.

  1. The table, which sits in the hallway, is used for correspondence.
  2. The car that crashed into the wall was blue.
  3. This is the woman, whose key you found.

Indefinite Pronouns :

Indefinite pronouns are used to represent an unspecified or unknown quantity of something. It generally refers to a person or thing that doesn't need to be specifically identified or has already been mentioned.

Examples of Indefinite pronouns are : other , none , some , anybody , everybody , no one

  1. Someone left their umbrella in the office.
  2. Everyone is invited to the party tomorrow night.
  3. Nobody knows the answer to the question.

Reflexive Pronouns

Reflexive pronouns are used when the subject and the object of a sentence are the same person or thing. They end in "-self" (singular) or "-selves" (plural).

Examples include myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, and themselves.

  1. She prepared the meal herself .
  2. The cat managed to open the door itself .
  3. They taught themselves how to code during the summer.

Interrogative Pronouns :

Interrogative pronouns are used to ask questions. These pronouns are used to inquire about people, objects, or information.

Examples include who, whom, whose, which, and what.

  1. Who is coming to the meeting today?
  2. What is your favorite color?
  3. Which movie do you want to watch?
